DAMAST

A Research System to Analyse Multi-Religious Constellations in the Islamicate World

About | Data Used | Features

DAMAST provides an interactive visualization to gain new insights on the multi-religious landscapes of the Middle East under Muslim rule during the Middle Ages (7th to 14th century). More than 8300 evidences of the presence of different religious communities at more than 442 places have been examined and recorded and are now accessible as a web-based geo-temporal multi-view system.

Although today—and today for the first time—, a mono-religious situation emerges in the Middle East, during the Medieval centuries, specific groups of Non-Muslims (especially Jews, Christians, and Zoroastrians) were tolerated and had in turn to accept a lower legal status, the so-called Dhimmi status. This led to great religious diversity in the Medieval Muslim world.Thus, in the many cities of the Muslim world, the divers Muslim strands as well as different churches of Eastern Christianity and Jewish traditions were living side by side. Often, three or even four Christian bishops resided in one city. Together, these religious groups formed the fabric of every-day life and culture in the Islamicate world. This grade of religious diversity is not found in the medieval cities of the Latin, Byzantine and Caucasian worlds, which were dominated by Christian rule.

However, the diachronic and synchronic complexity of these multi-religious populations is still understudied. Basic knowledge about the exact distribution and even the sheer existence of religious groups in many places is still lacking. So far, historical narratives are still based on impressions of singular sources and traditional views rather than empirical data.

Information from multiple sources can now be analyzed and compared, something not possible using texts, tables or static maps. Thus, traditional historiographical methods of heuristics and qualitative source analysis have been rethought and revived by embedding them into a new approach of visual analytics. At the same time, the project boosted methods of visual analytics and information visualization as, e.g., how to visually represent uncertainty and analytic provenance information. The project thus contributes to both disciplines, historiography and visualization research.

About

DAMAST offers the visualization software created and the data collected in the project Dhimmis and Muslims — Analysing Multi-Religious Spaces in the Medieval Muslim World, in short: DhiMu. DhiMu was carried out from 2016 until 2021 as a collaboration between the Institute for Medieval History II of Goethe University Frankfurt, led by Dorothea Weltecke (since October 2021: Professor for European History of the Middle Ages at Humboldt-Universität zu Berlin), and the Institute of Visualization and Interactive Systems of the University of Stuttgart. It was funded by the Volkswagen Foundation as part of the Mixed Methods program. Starting in October 2020, the project was also partially integrated in the Kollegforschungsgruppe “Polycentricity and Plurality of Premodern Christianity” (POLY) at Goethe University Frankfurt, funded by the DFG.

Scope of Time and Space

DAMAST visualizes evidence from the 7th to the 14th century in the area of the former Roman and Sassanian empires.

Choice of Places

Places included in the data were chosen with regard to their importance for the respective religious community:

  • buildings used for religious purposes: temples, mosques, churches, synagogues, etc.
  • sees of patriarchs, bishops, and other high officials in the church hierarchy in the case of Christianity
  • ### in the case of Judaism
  • cities recorded by al-Muqaddasī (metropolis: miṣr, capital of a province: kūra, or of a district: nāḥiya

Often, the exact location of a settlement has changed over time or is not exactly clear. One way DAMAST handles this problem is to assign a specific level of confidence to the localization (cf. the section on confidence below).

If it is not clear from a given source what group is meant, the general religious affiliation (e.g., Christianity) was recorded instead.

Tags

In addition to religious affiliation, time, and place, DAMAST implements tags to enhance the data and to enable more specific filtering of the database and visualization.

For instance, an evidence could be tagged as bishopric or metropolitan residence, enabling to visualize only places that are metropolitan sees rather than all Christian presence.

Confidence

DAMAST includes ambiguous information without distorting the visualization by attributing data with levels of confidence. (No attribution of confidence would either lead to the exclusion of data or to presenting uncertain information as seemingly equally certain.)

Five levels of confidence were used:

  • certain
  • probable
  • contested
  • uncertain
  • false

The following aspects of information were each attributed with a specific level of confidence:

  • Location – Is the place in the database at the provided location (i.e., geo-coordinates)?
  • Place attribution – Was the religious group present at this place, rather than another one, e.g., one with a similar name?
  • Time span – Was the religious group present during this time span?
  • Religion – Was it this religious group, rather than another one?
  • Source – Is the information provided by the source credible?
  • Interpretation – Do we interpret the information correctly?

Features

DAMAST offers several features, such as the visualization with its different views to filter and interactively display the data, the possibility to generate reports based on the current result of your research, and the collected information for each place in the database on so-called place URI pages.

Many of the controls of the visualization of DAMAST should be intuitive and easily understood through trial and error. Hovering with the mouse over a specific element will generally display a tooltip providing additional information. More specific and advanced aspects are best understood by reading the info texts. These can be accessed when clicking on the question mark symbol in the upper right corner of the respective view. An extensive documentation including the info texts as well as a introduction to the general principles of the visualization can be found here.

In what follows, only a brief overview is presented. In addition, video tutorials are provided.

Visualization

The principal object of DAMAST is to visualize the different aspects of the data such as religion, place, time, source, etc. as described above. The presentation is organized in different menus, so-called views, including a map, a timeline, and various lists and tables.

Each view is also used for filtering based on the different aspects of the data, allowing for a very fine-grained analysis. Applying the filters interactively changes the map and the rest of the visualized data (e.g., the figures given in the different views).

Apart from filtering the data, individual aspects can be temporarily brushed and linked, highlighting related data across all views.

In the Settings pane, different modes of the visualization can be switched. For instance, instead of only showing data matching the current filters, in All data mode data that has been filtered out is shown as well but in less saturated colors. Also, further features can be accessed (such as generating reports).

Place URI Pages

To provide all the information collected regarding a specific place, DAMAST offers a so-called place URI page for each place in the database. These can be accessed via the view Location List or through Places in the top menu.

A place URI page contains:

  • A box with an overview at the right side, including an interactive minimap, showing the position of the place, its main toponym and its geo-coordinates
  • A brief general description
  • Names – a list of the different toponyms of a town in different languages including Latin transcription
  • See Also – a list of external references
  • Religions – a list of all religions with their time of presence according to the respective sources
  • Sources – a list of these sources

Reports

In order to document, share, and reference the results when using DAMAST, reports can be generated. This is done either via the Settings view or through Docs > Create report from database in the top menu.

A report contains:

  • A description of all the filters applied (Query Description)
  • A note on how to cite the report
  • Separate lists of all evidences, places, and religions based on the filters
  • A representation of the timeline based on the filters
  • A list of all Sources underlying the Evidences

After generation, the report is presented as a static webpage with a permanent link. Each report has a Universally Unique Identifier (UUID), which is also part of this permanent link.

Note that the UUID or permanent link needs to be recorded by the user to access the report later!

From the report’s webpage, several other features are available, such as a PDF for offline use or sharing and a JSON file that contains the filters and can later be used to recreate the report.

The report can be re-run in case the underlying data of the visualization has changed. Note that in the case of this public instance of the tool, the data will not be expanded or substantially revised; only obvious errors will be corrected.

Also, the data underlying the report can be shown in the visualization; filters can thus be adapted and, e.g., a new, slightly altered report can be generated, based on the original one.
